EPIGENETICS · PULMONARY ARTERIAL HYPERTENSION

Epigenetic Age & Age Acceleration in PAH

Background. Pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) is a progressive vascular disease driven by mechanisms — metabolic dysfunction, inflammation, DNA damage, cellular senescence — that overlap heavily with the biology of aging itself. This raised a question: is PAH tissue simply older, biologically, than it should be?

Chronological age (based on birth date) and biological age can diverge. DNA methylation patterns at specific genomic loci change in a highly predictable way as cells age, which allows biological age — the epigenetic age — to be estimated directly from methylation data, independent of birth date. Comparing epigenetic age to chronological age gives the epigenetic age acceleration (EAA): an EAA above zero indicates the tissue is aging faster than expected, which is associated with increased cardiovascular and pulmonary disease risk.

What I did. I analyzed DNA methylation data from PAH patient samples, applying established epigenetic clock models (Horvath and Levine clocks) to compute epigenetic age for each sample, then calculated the epigenetic age acceleration by comparing it against each patient's chronological age. This was done both across different PAH-affected tissues from the same patients (distal pulmonary artery, lung, left ventricle, right ventricle) and in blood samples comparing PAH patients to controls, with statistical testing to determine whether the observed acceleration was significant.

What we found. Epigenetic age acceleration varied significantly across PAH-affected tissues within the same patients, and blood-based EAA was elevated in PAH patients relative to controls — suggesting that PAH is associated with a detectable acceleration of biological aging, distinct from the patient's actual age. This supports the idea that aging-related mechanisms are not just incidental to PAH but may be actively involved in its progression, and opens a path toward epigenetic age as a potential biomarker in the disease.

PHARMACOTRANSCRIPTOMICS · PULMONARY ARTERIAL HYPERTENSION

Drug Repurposing From Gene Expression Signatures in PAH

Background. PAH remodeling is driven by pulmonary artery smooth muscle cells (PASMCs) that adopt a hyperproliferative, apoptosis-resistant phenotype — a pattern that closely resembles cancer cell biology. Because of this overlap, and because no PAH- specific drug-signature database exists, connectivity-mapping tools built for oncology can still be leveraged to search for compounds that reverse the PAH gene expression pattern.

What I did. I analyzed gene expression data comparing PAH PASMCs to control PASMCs, and PAH lung tissue to control lung tissue, to identify differentially expressed genes (DEGs) that define the disease's molecular signature. I then used two connectivity- mapping databases — SigCom LINCS and LINCS L1000, both built primarily for cancer pharmacogenomics — to screen for compounds whose drug-induced expression signatures move in the opposite direction of the PAH DEG pattern, i.e., compounds predicted to reverse it.

Status. This identified a set of candidate compounds predicted to reverse the PAH pathological expression pattern, which are now being validated experimentally — in vitro, ex vivo, and in vivo. This work is still in progress.
